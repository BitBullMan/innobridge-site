2025 Crypto Crime Surged to $158 Billion — What It Means for AML Teams in 2026

The numbers are in: illicit cryptocurrency volume reached an all-time high of $158 billion in 2025, nearly tripling the previous year's figure. Yet here's the paradox — as a proportion of overall crypto market activity, illicit volume actually decreased from 1.3% to 1.2%. The crypto economy grew faster than the criminals could keep up.

For AML compliance teams, this creates a challenging reality. The absolute scale of illicit activity is larger than ever, but it's increasingly hidden in a much larger volume of legitimate transactions. Finding the needle in the haystack has never been harder — or more important.

The Bybit Breach Changed Everything

In 2025, threat actors stole $2.87 billion across nearly 150 separate hacking incidents — fewer than the previous year, but far more damaging per event. The Bybit breach alone accounted for $1.46 billion, representing 51% of all stolen funds for the year.

This concentration of damage in a single incident highlights a systemic vulnerability: centralized exchange infrastructure remains a high-value target, and the sophistication of attacks continues to escalate. For AML teams, this means that post-incident fund tracing capabilities need to be faster and more comprehensive than ever. When $1.46 billion moves through the blockchain ecosystem, the laundering operations that follow create ripple effects across dozens of chains and hundreds of intermediary wallets.

AI-Powered Scams: A 500% Surge

Perhaps the most alarming trend of 2025 was the 500% year-over-year increase in AI-driven scams. Threat actors now leverage generative AI to create convincing deepfake videos of executives, craft personalized phishing campaigns at scale, and build fraudulent investment platforms that are nearly indistinguishable from legitimate services.

Traditional rule-based screening systems struggle against these attacks because the patterns change rapidly. AI-generated content doesn't follow the playbooks that compliance teams have spent years learning to detect. This is pushing the industry toward more adaptive, behavior-based monitoring approaches — and making real-time risk scoring a necessity rather than a nice-to-have.

Regulatory Momentum: The GENIUS Act and Beyond

On the regulatory front, 2025 brought meaningful progress. The GENIUS Act, passed in July 2025, brought payment stablecoins under the Bank Secrecy Act for the first time, mandating comprehensive AML and sanctions compliance for stablecoin issuers. This is a significant development for the industry because stablecoins — particularly USDT and USDC — account for a substantial share of on-chain transaction volume and have been frequently used in laundering operations.

Meanwhile, the FATF continued to push for Travel Rule adoption globally. As of late 2025, 99 jurisdictions have adopted or are drafting legislation to enforce the Travel Rule for virtual asset transfers. However, only about 40 jurisdictions have achieved meaningful compliance — a reminder that passing laws and enforcing them are very different things.

What AML Teams Should Prioritize in 2026

Based on these trends, here are the capabilities that matter most right now:

  • Real-time address screening — With illicit flows at record levels, batch-processing risk checks is no longer sufficient. Every incoming address needs to be screened against up-to-date label databases before funds are accepted or released.
  • Cross-chain tracing capability — Criminals increasingly move funds across multiple chains using bridges, DEX aggregators, and privacy protocols. Investigations that stop at a single chain boundary will miss the full picture.
  • AI-aware monitoring — As social engineering attacks become AI-powered, compliance teams need tools that analyze behavioral patterns rather than relying solely on static rules or known-bad addresses.
  • Sanctions list integration — With stablecoins now formally under BSA requirements and OFAC maintaining an active crypto sanctions program, automated sanctions screening is table stakes.
  • Incident response speed — When a major breach occurs, the window for freezing funds shrinks by the hour. Teams need pre-built workflows and exchange cooperation channels to act quickly.

The Bottom Line

2025 proved that crypto crime is not a diminishing problem — it's an evolving one. The tools criminals use are becoming more sophisticated, the amounts are getting larger, and the laundering paths are growing more complex. But the intelligence infrastructure available to compliance teams is advancing as well.

The organizations that will navigate this landscape successfully are those investing in comprehensive label databases, real-time screening capabilities, and forensic tracing tools that work across the entire multi-chain ecosystem.

The scale of illicit activity is growing, but so is our ability to trace, attribute, and freeze it. The question for every VASP and financial institution is whether their compliance infrastructure is keeping pace.
